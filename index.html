Variation Spec: Know & Give Examples

Continuous and discontinuous variation

Individuals of the same species are not identical — they show variation. There are two types:

Continuous variation

Varies across a range with no distinct categories. Can take any value. Shown on a line graph or histogram.

  • Height
  • Mass / weight
  • Hand span
  • Foot length
  • Skin colour

Discontinuous variation

Falls into distinct groups with no intermediate values. Shown on a bar chart.

  • Blood group (A, B, AB, O)
  • Sex (male/female)
  • Tongue rolling
  • Attached/free earlobes
  • Eye colour

Inherited and environmental characteristics

Variation can be caused by genes (inherited from parents), environment, or — most commonly — a combination of both:

Inherited (genetic)EnvironmentalBoth
Blood group, sex, tongue rolling, eye colour, attached earlobes Language spoken, religion, tattoos, scars Height, skin colour, body mass, intelligence

Fertilisation & Implantation Spec: Know the Journey

Key terms

Gamete
A sex cell — sperm (male) or egg/ovum (female). Contains half the normal number of chromosomes.
Fertilisation
The fusion of a male gamete (sperm) with a female gamete (egg) to form a zygote.
Zygote
The single cell formed immediately after fertilisation. Contains a full set of chromosomes.
Embryo
The developing organism from the first cell divisions after fertilisation up to about 8 weeks.
Foetus
The developing organism from about 8 weeks after fertilisation until birth, when all major organs are formed.
Implantation
When the embryo embeds itself in the lining of the uterus wall, about 6–12 days after fertilisation.

The journey from fertilisation to implantation

Egg released from ovary
→
Sperm fertilises egg in oviduct
→
Zygote formed
→
Cell division begins (embryo)
→
Embryo travels to uterus
→
Implants in uterus wall

Fertilisation takes place in the oviduct (Fallopian tube). The fertilised egg divides repeatedly as it moves towards the uterus, becoming an embryo. It implants in the thick, spongy lining of the uterus — a process called implantation.

Development in the Uterus Spec: Placenta, Umbilical Cord, Amniotic Sac

Structures supporting foetal development

StructureFunction
Placenta A disc-shaped organ attached to the uterus wall. The foetal and maternal blood supplies run close together (but do NOT mix).
  • Oxygen and nutrients (glucose, amino acids) pass from mother → foetus
  • Carbon dioxide and urea (waste) pass from foetus → mother
  • Acts as a barrier against most pathogens (but not all)
  • Produces hormones to maintain the pregnancy
Umbilical cord Connects the foetus to the placenta. Contains blood vessels carrying oxygenated blood and nutrients to the foetus, and deoxygenated blood and waste back to the placenta.
Amniotic sac A tough, fluid-filled membrane surrounding the foetus.
Amniotic fluid The fluid inside the amniotic sac. It cushions and protects the foetus from physical shocks, maintains a constant temperature, and allows free movement.

Lifestyle and foetal development

The mother's lifestyle directly affects the foetus because some substances can cross the placenta:

  • Smoking — carbon monoxide and nicotine cross the placenta. CO binds to haemoglobin in the foetal blood, reducing oxygen delivery. This leads to low birth weight and increases risk of premature birth.
  • Alcohol — crosses the placenta freely. Can cause foetal alcohol syndrome — affecting brain development, causing learning difficulties and physical abnormalities.
  • Drugs — many prescribed and recreational drugs cross the placenta and can disrupt normal development.
  • Balanced diet — adequate folic acid, iron, calcium and protein are essential for healthy foetal growth.
  • Infections — some viruses (e.g. rubella/German measles) can cross the placenta and damage the foetus.
Puberty Spec: Changes During Puberty

What is puberty?

Puberty is the period during which the body develops from a child's into an adult's, becoming capable of sexual reproduction. It is triggered by sex hormones — testosterone in males, oestrogen in females.

Changes in both sexes

  • Growth spurt
  • Pubic and underarm hair grows
  • Skin becomes oilier (can cause acne)
  • Body odour increases (sweat glands more active)
  • Emotional and psychological changes

Male-specific changes

  • Testes and penis enlarge
  • Sperm production begins
  • Voice deepens ("voice breaks")
  • Facial and body hair grows
  • Muscles develop (broader shoulders)

Female-specific changes

  • Ovaries begin releasing eggs (ovulation begins)
  • Menstrual cycle begins (menarche)
  • Breasts develop
  • Hips widen (pelvis broadens)
The Menstrual Cycle Spec: Key Features from Diagrams

Overview

The menstrual cycle is a roughly 28-day repeating cycle that prepares the female body for pregnancy. It involves changes to the uterus lining and the release of an egg.

Menstruation
Days 1–5
The uterus lining breaks down and is shed along with blood through the vagina.
Proliferation
Days 6–13
The uterus lining rebuilds and thickens. Oestrogen is rising.
Ovulation
~Day 14
An egg is released from an ovary. The most fertile point of the cycle.
Secretory / Maintenance
Days 15–28
The lining is maintained by progesterone. If no fertilisation occurs, hormone levels fall and menstruation begins again.

Key features to identify from diagrams

  • Day 1 — start of menstruation (lining shed).
  • ~Day 14 — ovulation (egg release); peak oestrogen level on diagram.
  • Days 1–28 — the uterus lining thickness changes: thin during menstruation, thickest just before day 28.
  • Oestrogen — peaks around day 12–13, triggers ovulation.
  • Progesterone — rises after ovulation (days 15–26) to maintain the uterus lining; falls sharply at day 28 triggering menstruation.
  • If fertilisation occurs, progesterone remains high and menstruation does not happen — the cycle stops.
Exam skills: You may be given a diagram showing hormone levels and/or uterus lining thickness over 28 days and asked to: identify when ovulation occurs; state which hormone is rising or falling at a given point; explain what happens if fertilisation does or does not take place; predict what would happen to the cycle if pregnancy occurs.
